# EP-1106

## Problem Statement
Let $p(n)$ be the partition function and let
\[
F(n)=\omega\!\left(\prod_{1\le k\le n} p(k)\right)
\]
be the number of distinct prime factors appearing among $p(1),\dots,p(n)$.
Questions:
1. Does $F(n)\to\infty$?
2. Is $F(n)>n$ for all sufficiently large $n$?

## Literature
- First question is resolved positively.
- Known lower bounds include at least logarithmic growth.
- Second stronger inequality remains open.

## Our Approaches / What Is Proven
- Added standalone deep modular scan (`scripts/ep1106.mjs`).
- For each prime $q\le Q$, computed first index $k\le N$ such that
\[
q\mid p(k)
\]
via Euler pentagonal recurrence modulo $q$.
- Also computed full residue-zero profiles up to $N$ for a substantial prime subset (no early exit) to deepen computation and capture recurrence behavior.

## Computation-Guided Observations
Deeper run used:
- $N=50{,}000$,
- $Q=12{,}000$,
- tested primes: $1{,}438$.

Results:
- among primes $\le 12{,}000$, exactly $1{,}433$ were detected dividing some $p(k)$ with $k\le 50{,}000$,
- giving rigorous lower bound
\[
F(50{,}000)\ge 1{,}433.
\]
- this remains far below $n$ at this scale (so finite data does not support the stronger eventual inequality).

Interpretation:
- strong finite evidence for robust growth of $F(n)$,
- but still only lower-bound evidence from bounded prime range and finite $n$.

## Status
- First question solved in literature.
- Second question open.
- Added deeper standalone modular recurrence computation producing large explicit lower-bound profiles.

## Added Proof Layer (No New Computation)

### First Question (Closed)
Question 1 asks whether
\[
F(n)=\omega\!\left(\prod_{k\le n}p(k)\right)\to\infty.
\]
This follows from the cited literature results giving unconditional growth
lower bounds for the number of distinct prime divisors appearing among
$p(1),\dots,p(n)$ (in particular, unbounded growth).

Therefore Question 1 is resolved affirmatively.

### Second Question (Open)
Question 2 asks for the much stronger eventual inequality
\[
F(n)>n\quad\text{for all sufficiently large }n.
\]

### Blocking Lemma
- Need a near-linear lower bound of form
  \[
  F(n)\ge (1+\delta)n
  \]
  eventually (or an equivalent structural statement forcing at least one new
  prime divisor at average rate $>1$ per index).

Existing methods establish unbounded/logarithmic-type growth, but not this
near-linear regime.

## Deeper Proof Program (A => B => C)

### Target
Address whether eventually
\[
F(n)>n.
\]

### Step A: Prime-Divisor Arrival Rate
Define \(\Delta F(n)=F(n)-F(n-1)\). Obtain lower bounds on averaged
\(\sum_{m\le n}\Delta F(m)\) over short blocks of length \(L\), with mean
\(>1\) or near \(1\) plus positive drift.

### Step B: Drift Stabilization
Show negative fluctuations are too sparse to cancel Step A drift on long scales.

### Step C: Eventual Domination
From cumulative drift deduce \(F(n)-n\to +\infty\) (or at least \(>0\) eventually).

### Concrete Blocking Lemma (Most Critical)
Need a theorem giving frequent introduction of new prime divisors of \(p(n)\)
at density \(>1\) per index on average, not merely unboundedly many over very
sparse subsequences.
