# EP-928

## Problem Statement
Let $\alpha,\beta\in(0,1)$ and let $P(n)$ be the largest prime factor of $n$.
Does the natural density of integers $n$ such that
$$
P(n)<n^{\alpha},\qquad P(n+1)<(n+1)^{\beta}
$$
exist?

## Literature
- Dickman gives marginal smooth-number density via $\rho(1/\alpha)$.
- Logarithmic-density versions and conditional natural-density results are known in parts of the literature.
- Full unconditional natural density in this two-variable setting remains open in general.

## Our Approaches / What Is Proven
- Added standalone deep empirical scan with:
  - sieve-based exact largest-prime-factor table up to $N=30{,}000{,}000$,
  - dense $(\alpha,\beta)$ grid (81 pairs),
  - checkpoint convergence at $N=5,10,20,30$ million.
- Recorded joint density, product of marginals, covariance surrogate $(\text{joint}-\text{product})$, and ratio joint/product.

## Computation-Guided Observations
- Runtime for this deeper run: about $6.2$ seconds.
- Persistent mild negative correlation across tested pairs: joint density stays below product density.
- Example at $N=30{,}000{,}000$:
  - $(\alpha,\beta)=(0.5,0.5)$: joint/product $\approx 0.9263$.
  - $(0.7,0.7)$: joint/product $\approx 0.9851$.
  - $(0.8,0.8)$: joint/product $\approx 0.9903$.
- As thresholds become less restrictive, ratio approaches $1$ from below, consistent with near-independence at higher smoothness exponents.
