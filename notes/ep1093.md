## Deeper Proof Program (A => B => C)

### Target
Control the deficiency distribution for binomial divisibility (especially whether high-deficiency cases are finite or infinite).

### A. Concrete Blocking Lemma (Most Critical)

Prove that deficiency \(\ge 2\) forces a rigid base-\(p\) carry pattern across at least two primes, restricting \((n,k)\) to a union of thin parametric families with convergent counting series.

### B. Reduction Lemmas (with proofs)

#### Lemma B1 (Kummer carry encoding)
For prime \(p\), \(v_p\binom{n}{k}\) equals the number of carries when adding \(k\) and \(n-k\) in base \(p\).

**Proof.**
This is Kummer’s theorem. \(\square\)

#### Lemma B2 (deficiency implies multi-prime carry scarcity)
If deficiency is large, then for many small primes \(p\), the carry count pattern must be near-minimal.

**Proof.**
Large deficiency means unexpectedly weak prime divisibility profile of \(\binom nk\). By Lemma B1 this translates to sparse carry events simultaneously across primes. \(\square\)

#### Lemma B3 (family counting transfer)
If admissible carry-pattern families are counted by \(O(x^\theta)\), \(\theta<2\), among \(n\le x\), then high-deficiency pairs are sparse (and finite if \(\theta<1\) after second-parameter summation).

**Proof.**
Sum family bounds over \(k\)-ranges compatible with each family; sparsity/finiteness follows from exponent summation. \(\square\)

### C. Main unresolved point

The hard gap is a uniform classification of simultaneous carry constraints across several primes with explicit counting bounds.
