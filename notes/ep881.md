# EP-881

## Problem Statement
Let $A\subset\mathbb N$ be a minimal additive basis of order $k$ (removing any infinite subset destroys order-$k$ basis property). Must there exist an infinite $B\subset A$ such that $A\setminus B$ is a basis of order $k+1$?

## Literature
- The repository source had malformed metadata around this entry, but the mathematical statement above is recoverable from the normalized catalog text.

## Our Approaches / What Is Proven
- Built a finite-horizon analog for $k=2\to3$:
  - search for finite minimal 2-bases on intervals $[L,U]$,
  - test whether deleting a small subset yields a 3-basis on the same interval.

## Computation-Guided Observations
- In tested regimes, finite minimal 2-bases were found repeatedly.
- In a substantial fraction of found instances, removing a small subset (often size 1) produced a finite-horizon 3-basis.
- This is suggestive finite behavior only; it does not resolve the infinite theorem.
