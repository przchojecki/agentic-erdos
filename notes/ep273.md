## Deeper Proof Program (A => B => C)

### Target
Decide whether one can cover all sufficiently large integers using one residue
class modulo each \(p-1\) (prime \(p\ge5\)) as required in EP-273.

### Step A: Density-balance necessary conditions
Quantify harmonic mass of chosen classes and derive unavoidable lower bounds for
uncovered density if choices are too sparse.

### Step B: Chinese-remainder compatibility
Analyze finite intersections of chosen congruence classes and their periodic
structure.

### Step C: Infinite covering criterion
Pass from periodic finite-level cover properties to eventual global coverage.

### Concrete Blocking Lemma (Most Critical)
Need a local-to-global covering lemma for infinitely many moduli \(p-1\): if
every large interval has a bounded-length fully covered subinterval pattern at
periodic truncations, then full eventual coverage follows.

## Lemma Chain (with Proofs)

### Lemma 1 (Finite-modulus periodicity)
Fix moduli \(m_1,\dots,m_t\) and residues \(r_i\). The covered set
\[
U_t:=\bigcup_{i=1}^t \{n:\ n\equiv r_i\pmod{m_i}\}
\]
is periodic modulo \(M_t:=\mathrm{lcm}(m_1,\dots,m_t)\).

\[
\textit{Proof.}
\]
Each congruence class is periodic mod \(m_i\), hence mod \(M_t\). Finite unions
of \(M_t\)-periodic sets are \(M_t\)-periodic.
\(\square\)

### Lemma 2 (Coverage fraction in one period)
For finite \(t\), asymptotic density of \(U_t\) equals
\[
\frac{|U_t\cap[1,M_t]|}{M_t}.
\]

\[
\textit{Proof.}
\]
By Lemma 1, indicator of \(U_t\) repeats every \(M_t\), so block averages over
full periods are constant and equal to period average.
\(\square\)

### Lemma 3 (Reduction to truncation ladder)
Suppose there is an increasing truncation sequence \(t_j\) such that the
complements \(U_{t_j}^c\) in one period have maximal gap length \(g_j\) with
\(g_j\to 0\) relative to scale, and the chosen residues are consistent across
truncations. Then eventual full coverage follows.

\[
\textit{Proof sketch.}
\]
Consistency prevents previously covered points from being lost. Shrinking maximal
uncovered gaps implies every sufficiently large integer is covered at some stage,
hence in the limit union.
\(\square\)
