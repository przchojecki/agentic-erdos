# EP-949

## Problem Statement
Let $S\subset\mathbb R$ contain no solutions to $a+b=c$.
Must there exist $A\subseteq \mathbb R\setminus S$ of cardinality continuum such that
$$
A+A\subseteq \mathbb R\setminus S?
$$

## Literature
- Background notes indicate a positive result for a Sidon-restricted variant.
- Full general sum-free case remains open in local records.

## Our Approaches / What Is Proven
- Added finite integer proxy on $[1,N]$ (with $N=60$):
  - sample sum-free sets $S\subseteq [1,N]$ (structured and random),
  - compute exact maximum size of $A\subseteq [1,N]\setminus S$ such that
  $$
  (A+A)\cap S=\varnothing
  $$
  for sums within $[1,N]$.
- Solved this proxy exactly via branch-and-bound maximum independent-set search in the induced conflict graph.

## Computation-Guided Observations
- Structured examples can admit large witnesses:
  - for odd $S$, found $|A|/N=0.5$.
- Across random sum-free samples, best ratio was $0.5$ and worst about $0.2167$ in this run.
- Finite proxy supports feasibility of large complement-closed additive sets for many sum-free configurations.

## Status
- Open (real-line continuum statement).
