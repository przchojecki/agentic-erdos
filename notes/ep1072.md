# EP-1072

## Problem Statement
For each prime $p$, let $f(p)$ be the least $n$ such that
\[
n!\equiv -1\pmod p.
\]
Study asymptotic behavior of $f(p)/p$ and of the event $f(p)=p-1$.

## Literature
- Problem remains open regarding limit/distribution behavior.

## Our Approaches / What Is Proven
- Replaced batch stub by a standalone exact factorial-residue scan.
- Deep run computed $f(p)$ exactly for all primes
\[
p\le 220{,}000,
\]
with full inner modular-factorial progression per prime.

## Deeper Proof Program (A => B => C)

### Target
Understand distribution of
\[
\frac{f(p)}{p},\qquad f(p):=\min\{n:\ n!\equiv -1\pmod p\},
\]
and the event \(f(p)=p-1\).

### A. Concrete Blocking Lemma (Most Critical)

Show a positive-proportion lower bound for primes with
\[
f(p)\le \alpha p
\]
for some fixed \(\alpha<1\), together with an upper tail bound excluding mass near \(1\) beyond a controlled amount.

This would force nontrivial distributional behavior of \(f(p)/p\).

### B. Reduction Lemmas (with proofs)

#### Lemma B1 (inverse-factorial reformulation)
For prime \(p\), \(f(p)\) is the least \(n\) such that
\[
\prod_{k=n+1}^{p-1}k \equiv 1 \pmod p.
\]

**Proof.**
From Wilson, \((p-1)!\equiv -1\pmod p\). Divide by \(n!\) (invertible mod \(p\)) to get \(\prod_{k=n+1}^{p-1}k\equiv (-1)(n!)^{-1}\). Requiring \(n!\equiv -1\) is equivalent to the product being \(1\). \(\square\)

#### Lemma B2 (trivial endpoint witness)
\[
f(p)\le p-1,\quad\text{and}\quad f(p)=p-1\iff (p-2)!\not\equiv -1\pmod p.
\]

**Proof.**
\((p-1)!\equiv -1\) always gives an admissible \(n=p-1\). Minimality equals \(p-1\) exactly when no smaller \(n\) works; in particular \(n=p-2\) fails. \(\square\)

#### Lemma B3 (A implies nondegenerate limiting behavior)
If Lemma A gives two separated positive-mass regions for \(f(p)/p\), then \(f(p)/p\) cannot converge to a constant.

**Proof.**
Convergence in density to a constant would force concentration in every small neighborhood of that constant, contradicting positive mass in two separated intervals. \(\square\)

### C. Main unresolved point

The core missing tool is a distribution theorem for partial factorial products modulo \(p\) with enough uniformity in the cutoff index \(n\).
