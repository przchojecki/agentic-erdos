# EP-634

## Problem Statement
Find all \(n\) such that there exists at least one triangle that can be cut into \(n\) congruent triangles.

## Literature
- Known positive families include \(n^2\), \(2n^2\), \(3n^2\), \(6n^2\), and \(n^2+m^2\).
- Recent work (Zhang, 2025) provides large additional infinite families of the form \(n^2ab\) above explicit thresholds.
- Small cases like \(7,11\) are known non-representable; \(19\) is a canonical unresolved value.

## Our Approaches / What Is Proven
- Implemented a deeper standalone arithmetic coverage scan up to \(N=12000\) using known constructive families.
- Tracked missing-set growth and prime \(4k+3\) behavior inside this known-family model.

## Computation-Guided Observations
- In this model, \(7,11,19,23,31\) remain missing, while \(27\) is represented.
- Missing density in known families is still high over tested range (about \(0.709\) at \(N=12000\)).
- The fraction of missing primes \(p\equiv 3 \pmod 4\) in this model is near \(1\) at tested scales (about \(0.999\) by \(N=12000\)).
- This is evidence for arithmetic sparsity of currently known constructions, not a proof of true non-representability.

## Status
- Open.
- Full characterization of representable \(n\) is unresolved.
