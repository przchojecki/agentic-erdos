## Deeper Proof Program (A => B => C)

### Target
Determine true growth rate of
\[
f(n)=\inf_{a_1<\cdots<a_n}\max_{|z|=1}\prod_{j=1}^n |1-z^{a_j}|,
\]
after the failure of \(\log f(n)\gg n^c\).

### Step A: Harmonic-analytic upper bounds
Control logarithmic integrals and peaks of the trigonometric product.

### Step B: Extremal exponent-pattern structure
Characterize minimizing exponent sets \( \{a_j\} \) up to additive-combinatorial
equivalence.

### Step C: Tight asymptotic law
Match constructive upper bounds with universal lower bounds.

### Concrete Blocking Lemma (Most Critical)
Need a min-max inequality linking \(L^\infty\)-norm of the product to additive
energy/profile of the exponent set.

## LaTeX Proofs for A => B => C Skeleton

### Proposition DC1 (Log-product decomposition)
For \(|z|=1\), \(z=e^{it}\),
\[
\log \prod_{j=1}^n |1-z^{a_j}| = \sum_{j=1}^n \log|2\sin(a_j t/2)|.
\]

\[
\textit{Proof.}
\]
Use \(|1-e^{i\theta}|=2|\sin(\theta/2)|\).
\(\square\)

### Proposition DC2 (Reduction)
If one derives uniform upper/lower asymptotics for the supremum of the RHS in DC1
over \(t\), optimized over \(\{a_j\}\), then asymptotics of \(f(n)\) follow by
exponentiation.

\[
\textit{Proof.}
\]
By definition of \(f(n)\) as min of max product.
\(\square\)
