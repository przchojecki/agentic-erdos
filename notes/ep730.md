# EP-730 deeper attempt

## Statement
Are there infinitely many `n!=m` such that `C(2n,n)` and `C(2m,m)` have the same prime-divisor set?

## Rigorous reduction used in this attempt
Let `B_n = C(2n,n)`. Then

`B_{n+1} = B_n * 2(2n+1)/(n+1)`.

So support change from `n` to `n+1` is controlled exactly by prime factors of `2n+1` and `n+1`.
For the special family `n=2^m-1`, this becomes

`B_{2^m} = B_{2^m-1} * (2^{m+1}-1)/2^{m-1}`.

Hence odd-prime support equality for `(2^m-1,2^m)` is equivalent to:
every odd prime divisor of `2^{m+1}-1` already divides `B_{2^m-1}`.

## Computation in this attempt
I ran exact support-tracking from the recurrence (not heuristic hashing) at large scale.

Data file:
- `data/ep730_large_consecutive_scan.json`
- `data/ep726_ep730_ep731_scan.json` (`ep730` section, small-range cross-check)

Findings:
- Up to `n=2,500,000`, found `25,547` consecutive pairs `(n,n+1)` with equal support.
- Density in buckets stabilizes near `~1%` (for example `20,320` hits up to `2,000,000`).
- First hits recover known small examples (`87,88`, `607,608`) and continue densely afterward.

## Interpretation
This is very strong finite evidence for infinitude, but still not a proof.
Hard point: turning the local recurrence support conditions into a deterministic infinite-family
construction (or a theorem guaranteeing infinitely many `n` with no support toggles).
