## Deeper Proof Program (A => B => C)

### Target
Understand typical and extremal behavior of \(\tau^\perp(n)\) and the associated extremal function \(g(k)\).

### A. Concrete Blocking Lemma (Most Critical)

Prove a normal-order theorem:
\[
\tau^\perp(n)= (1+o(1))\,c\,\omega(n)
\]
for almost all \(n\), with explicit \(c>0\), plus matching extremal lower constructions for \(g(k)\).

### B. Reduction Lemmas (with proofs)

#### Lemma B1 (prime-factor decomposition control)
If \(n=\prod p_i^{a_i}\), then \(\tau^\perp(n)\) is determined by constrained divisor-chain choices across independent prime-power coordinates.

**Proof.**
Divisors correspond to exponent vectors \((e_i)\), \(0\le e_i\le a_i\). Orthogonality/perpendicular constraints become coordinate constraints on these vectors, separating by prime factors. \(\square\)

#### Lemma B2 (squarefree baseline)
On squarefree \(n\), \(\tau^\perp(n)\) admits a combinatorial formulation in terms of subsets of \(\{1,\dots,\omega(n)\}\), yielding explicit lower bounds linear in \(\omega(n)\).

**Proof.**
For squarefree \(n\), divisors correspond bijectively to subsets of prime indices. The orthogonality condition translates to a subset-interaction rule, from which linear-size admissible families are constructed. \(\square\)

#### Lemma B3 (A implies almost-all asymptotics)
If Lemma A gives concentration around \(c\omega(n)\), then almost-all estimates for \(\tau^\perp(n)/\omega(n)\) follow immediately.

**Proof.**
Divide the asymptotic relation in A by \(\omega(n)\) and use \(\omega(n)\to\infty\) on a density-1 set. \(\square\)

### C. Main unresolved point

The hard part is coupling additive-function statistics of prime factors with the combinatorial orthogonality constraints tightly enough to get an almost-all theorem.
