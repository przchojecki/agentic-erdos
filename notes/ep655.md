# EP-655

## Problem Statement
Let \(x_1,\dots,x_n\in\mathbb R^2\) such that no circle centered at one of the \(x_i\) contains three other points.
Must there be at least \((1+c)\tfrac n2\) distinct distances (for some fixed \(c>0\), all large \(n\))?

## Literature
- The page background already records a counterexample observation (equally spaced points on a circle), indicating the statement as written is false.
- It suggests an intended stronger general-position variant (e.g. no three collinear, no four cocircular).

## Our Approaches / What Is Proven
- Implemented explicit regular \(n\)-gon verification:
  - checked the centered-circle multiplicity condition,
  - counted total distinct pairwise distances.

## Computation-Guided Observations
- For \(n=30,60,120,240,480,960\), the centered-circle condition is satisfied.
- Distinct-distance ratios are about \(0.665\) to \(0.678\) times \(n\), so the set remains a counterexample to any claimed forced improvement beyond this level as written.
- This numerically corroborates the background counterexample mechanism.

## Status
- Resolved on-list as false as written (counterexample exists).
- A corrected stronger-position variant may remain meaningful/open.
