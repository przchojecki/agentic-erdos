# EP-913

## Problem Statement
Are there infinitely many $n$ such that if
$$
n(n+1)=\prod_i p_i^{k_i},
$$
then all exponents $k_i$ are distinct?

## Literature
- Existing background notes mention a conditional route via prime-pattern heuristics (e.g. constructions yielding exponent pattern $\{1,2,3\}$ under additional primality assumptions), but no unconditional infinitude proof is known here.

## Our Approaches / What Is Proven
- Performed deep finite scan up to $N=5\cdot 10^7$.
- For each $n\le N$, factored both $n$ and $n+1$ exactly and checked whether exponent multiset in $n(n+1)$ has pairwise distinct entries.
- Tracked witness counts, first witnesses, and checkpoint densities.

## Computation-Guided Observations
- Found $1149$ witness values up to $5\times10^7$.
- Empirical witness density up to this range is about
$$
2.298\times 10^{-5}.
$$
- Early witnesses include many structured patterns (e.g. exponent sets $\{1,2,3\}$ appearing repeatedly).
- Finite data supports existence of many witnesses but does not decide infinitude.
