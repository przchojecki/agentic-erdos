## Statement
Are there only finitely many powers of `2` whose base-`3` expansion uses only
digits `{0,1}`?

## Deeper Proof Program (A => B => C)

### Target
Prove finiteness of exponents \(n\) such that base-3 expansion of \(2^n\) uses only digits \(\{0,1\}\).

### A. Concrete Blocking Lemma (Most Critical)

Establish an \(S\)-unit gap lemma: if
\[
2^n=\sum_{j=0}^{m}\varepsilon_j 3^j,\qquad \varepsilon_j\in\{0,1\},
\]
then linear forms in logarithms force \(n\) below an explicit bound except finitely many exceptional patterns.

### B. Reduction Lemmas (with proofs)

#### Lemma B1 (digit equation form)
Digit restriction \(\{0,1\}\) is equivalent to representation in B1 equation above.

**Proof.**
Base-3 expansion coefficients are exactly ternary digits. \(\square\)

#### Lemma B2 (modular periodic constraints)
For each modulus \(M\), residues of \(2^n\pmod{3^M}\) are periodic in \(n\), giving strong compatibility conditions on low ternary digits.

**Proof.**
\(2\) is invertible modulo \(3^M\), so powers are periodic with period dividing \(\varphi(3^M)\). \(\square\)

#### Lemma B3 (A implies finiteness)
If A gives explicit bound on \(n\), only finitely many exponents remain to check.

**Proof.**
Finite search on bounded \(n\)-range completes proof. \(\square\)

### C. Main unresolved point

The bottleneck is sharpening transcendence bounds enough to beat combinatorial growth of admissible digit patterns.
