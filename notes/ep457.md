# EP-457 partial resolution

## Statement
Is there `epsilon>0` with infinitely many `n` such that every prime
`p <= (2+epsilon) log n` divides `prod_{1<=i<=log n}(n+i)`?

## What is resolved
Background gives a construction yielding
`q(n,log n) >= (2+o(1)) log n` for infinitely many `n`, where `q` is the least
missing prime divisor.

## What remains open in this note
The strict improvement to `(2+epsilon) log n` is not proved by the cited
construction.

## Status
- threshold at `2+o(1)`: known.
- fixed `2+epsilon` improvement infinitely often: unresolved.

## Deeper Proof Program (A => B => C)

### Target
Upgrade known \(2+o(1)\) threshold to fixed \(2+\varepsilon\) infinitely often for least missing prime \(q(n,\lfloor\log n\rfloor)\).

### A. Concrete Blocking Lemma (Most Critical)

Prove a prime-covering concentration lemma: for infinitely many \(n\), primes
\[
p\le (2+\varepsilon)\log n
\]
all divide \(\prod_{1\le i\le \log n}(n+i)\), i.e. residue classes of \(-i\) mod \(p\) are fully hit in the short interval.

### B. Reduction Lemmas (with proofs)

#### Lemma B1 (least-missing-prime definition)
\[
q(n,k)=\min\{p\ \text{prime}: p\nmid \prod_{1\le i\le k}(n+i)\}.
\]

**Proof.**
Definition. \(\square\)

#### Lemma B2 (equivalence to full divisibility below threshold)
\[
q(n,k)>(2+\varepsilon)\log n
\iff
\forall p\le (2+\varepsilon)\log n,\ p\mid \prod_{i=1}^{k}(n+i).
\]

**Proof.**
By minimality in B1. \(\square\)

#### Lemma B3 (A implies infinitely-often claim)
If A holds for infinitely many \(n\) with \(k=\lfloor\log n\rfloor\), then \(q(n,\log n)/( \log n)\ge2+\varepsilon\) infinitely often.

**Proof.**
Apply B2 on those \(n\). \(\square\)

### C. Main unresolved point

The bottleneck is proving simultaneous small-prime divisibility across very short consecutive blocks with fixed positive margin above \(2\).
