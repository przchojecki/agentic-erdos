# EP-849

## Problem Statement
For each integer $t\ge 1$, does there exist an integer $a$ such that
$$\binom{n}{k}=a\quad (1\le k\le n/2)$$
has exactly $t$ solutions?

## Literature
- This is the Singmaster-type multiplicity problem.
- Known examples:
  - multiplicity $3$ (e.g. $a=120$),
  - multiplicity $4$ (e.g. $a=3003$).
- No values of multiplicity $\ge 5$ are known in the classical discussion.

## Our Approaches / What Is Proven
- Performed deeper finite scan of Pascal interior values up to row $n\le 1500$.
- Counted multiplicity of each value among coefficients $\binom{n}{k}$ with $1\le k\le n/2$.

## Computation-Guided Observations
- Maximum multiplicity found in this range is still $3$.
- Histogram in this window:
  - multiplicity 1: 562,325 values,
  - multiplicity 2: 83 values,
  - multiplicity 3: 3 values,
  - multiplicity $\ge 4$: none observed.
- This remains consistent with rarity of high multiplicities in finite ranges.
