## Deeper Proof Program (A => B => C)

### Target
Prove both infinitely many \(n\) with \((a_n,L_n)=1\) and infinitely many with
\((a_n,L_n)>1\) for the EP-291 sequence setup.

### Step A: Divisibility recurrence extraction
Express \(L_n\) through previous terms and isolate congruence classes forcing
common factors.

### Step B: Coprime subsequence mechanism
Construct infinitely many indices where all prime factors of \(L_n\) are excluded
from \(a_n\) by congruence/valuation control.

### Step C: Coexistence proof
Combine Step A and B to establish infinitude of both regimes.

### Concrete Blocking Lemma (Most Critical)
Need a uniform local-sieving lemma on \(a_n\) modulo prime factors of \(L_n\)
that produces infinitely many simultaneous non-divisibility events.

## Lemma Chain (with Proofs)

### Lemma 1 (Easy \(>1\) infinitude route)
If infinitely many \(n\) satisfy \(p\mid a_n\) and \(p\mid L_n\) for some prime
\(p\), then \((a_n,L_n)>1\) infinitely often.

\[
\textit{Proof.}
\]
By definition of gcd, a shared prime divisor implies gcd \(>1\).
\(\square\)

### Lemma 2 (Coprime criterion)
If for infinitely many \(n\), every prime \(p\mid L_n\) satisfies \(p\nmid a_n\),
then \((a_n,L_n)=1\) infinitely often.

\[
\textit{Proof.}
\]
No common prime factors means gcd equals \(1\).
\(\square\)

### Lemma 3 (Reduction)
EP-291 reduces to proving the hypothesis of Lemma 2, since the \(>1\) direction
is already routine via Lemma 1.

\[
\textit{Proof.}
\]
One side is easy/known; the difficult side is exactly Lemma 2's condition.
\(\square\)
