# EP-1052

## Problem Statement
Search for unitary perfect numbers, i.e. integers $n$ with
\[
\sigma^*(n)=2n,
\]
where $\sigma^*$ is the sum of unitary divisors.

## Literature
- Classical list of known examples is very short; odd unitary-perfect examples are unknown.

## Our Approaches / What Is Proven
- Added standalone exact scan using SPF-based factorization and multiplicative formula
\[
\sigma^*(n)=\prod_{p^a\parallel n}(p^a+1).
\]
- Deepened range to
\[
n\le 20{,}000{,}000.
\]

## Computation-Guided Observations
- Hits up to this bound are exactly
\[
6,\ 60,\ 90,\ 87360.
\]
- No odd hits were found.

## Status
- Global finiteness/infinitude questions remain open.
- Added deeper standalone exact-range verification.

## Deeper Proof Program (A => B => C)

### Target
Classify all unitary perfect numbers \(n\) with
\[
\sigma^*(n)=2n,\qquad \sigma^*(n)=\prod_{p^a\parallel n}(p^a+1).
\]

### A. Concrete Blocking Lemma (Most Critical)

Prove a prime-exponent rigidity bound: in any unitary perfect \(n\), the set of prime powers \(p^a\parallel n\) with \((p^a+1)\) carrying large odd prime factors is uniformly bounded, forcing only finitely many admissible multiplicative templates.

### B. Reduction Lemmas (with proofs)

#### Lemma B1 (ratio factorization)
\[
\frac{\sigma^*(n)}{n}=\prod_{p^a\parallel n}\left(1+\frac1{p^a}\right).
\]

**Proof.**
Divide multiplicative formula for \(\sigma^*(n)\) by \(n=\prod p^a\). \(\square\)

#### Lemma B2 (odd-case parity obstruction)
If \(n\) is odd, then every factor \(1+1/p^a\) has even numerator, imposing high 2-adic demand in product equal to \(2\).

**Proof.**
For odd \(p^a\), \(p^a+1\) is even. Product numerator accumulates powers of \(2\) that must be exactly balanced by denominator cancellation; this is highly restrictive. \(\square\)

#### Lemma B3 (A implies finiteness)
If Lemma A bounds admissible templates, only finitely many \(n\) solve \(\sigma^*(n)=2n\).

**Proof.**
Each template yields a finite Diophantine search over bounded prime-power patterns. Finite union remains finite. \(\square\)

### C. Main unresolved point

The core obstacle is controlling simultaneous prime-factor interactions among terms \(p^a+1\), where cyclotomic divisibility creates long dependency chains.
