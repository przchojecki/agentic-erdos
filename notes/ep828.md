# EP-828

## Problem Statement
Is it true that for every integer $a$ there are infinitely many $n$ such that
$$\varphi(n)\mid (n+a)?$$

## Literature
- Conjectured by Graham.
- Special related cases:
  - $\varphi(n)\mid n-1$ ties to Lehmer-type questions;
  - $\varphi(n)\mid n$ has classical explicit characterization.
- The full all-shifts infinitude statement remains open.

## Our Approaches / What Is Proven
- Ran deeper exact finite scan up to $N=2\cdot 10^6$.
- For each shift $a\in\{-16,-12,-8,-4,-1,0,1,2,3,5,8,12,16\}$:
  - counted solutions to $\varphi(n)\mid n+a$;
  - recorded early solutions and largest observed gaps.

## Computation-Guided Observations
- Strong shift asymmetry persists at larger range:
  - negative shifts (especially $a=-1,-4$) are much denser;
  - small positive shifts remain very sparse.
- Examples:
  - for $a=-1$: $148{,}933$ hits up to $2\cdot10^6$;
  - for $a=1$: only $5$ hits in this range.
- This supports that any general proof will need strongly shift-specific structure.
