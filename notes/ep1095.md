# EP-1095

## Problem Statement
Let $g(k)>k+1$ be the smallest $n$ such that every prime divisor of
\[
\binom{n}{k}
\]
is greater than $k$.
Estimate $g(k)$.

## Literature
- Classical bounds show very rapid growth and strong irregularity.
- Heuristic viewpoint suggests
\[
\log g(k)\asymp \frac{k}{\log k}
\]
with substantial oscillation.

## Our Approaches / What Is Proven
- Replaced stub with standalone exact search (`scripts/ep1095.mjs`) using Lucas-theorem divisibility tests:
  - for each prime $p\le k$, test whether $p\mid\binom{n}{k}$ via base-$p$ digit comparison,
  - accept the first $n$ where all such tests are nondivisibility.
- This is exact for all found values within the chosen cap.

## Deeper Proof Program (A => B => C)

### Target
Estimate growth of \(g(k)\), the first \(n>k+1\) with all prime divisors of \(\binom nk\) exceeding \(k\).

### A. Concrete Blocking Lemma (Most Critical)

Prove that for most \(k\), there is \(n\le \exp(Ck/\log k)\) such that
\[
p\nmid\binom nk\quad\forall p\le k.
\]
This would establish a near-heuristic upper envelope for \(g(k)\).

### B. Reduction Lemmas (with proofs)

#### Lemma B1 (prime-threshold equivalence)
\[
g(k)=\min\{n>k+1:\ p\nmid\binom nk\ \forall p\le k\}.
\]

**Proof.**
All prime factors of \(\binom nk\) being \(>k\) is equivalent to nondivisibility by every prime \(p\le k\). \(\square\)

#### Lemma B2 (Lucas digit constraint)
For prime \(p\le k\),
\[
p\nmid\binom nk
\iff
\text{every base-}p\text{ digit of }k \le \text{corresponding digit of }n.
\]

**Proof.**
Directly Lucas theorem mod \(p\). \(\square\)

#### Lemma B3 (CRT digit-synthesis strategy)
If one can choose \(n\) whose base-\(p\) digits dominate those of \(k\) simultaneously for all \(p\le k\), then \(n\) is admissible and yields an upper bound for \(g(k)\).

**Proof.**
Apply Lemma B2 for each \(p\le k\); all conditions together imply the threshold property by Lemma B1. \(\square\)

### C. Main unresolved point

The obstruction is simultaneous control of digit-dominance constraints across many small primes without making \(n\) too large.
