# EP-668

## Problem Statement
Let \(u(n)\) be the maximum number of unit distances among \(n\) points in \(\mathbb R^2\).
How many incongruent \(n\)-point sets attain \(u(n)\)?
Does this count tend to infinity with \(n\)? Is it always \(>1\) for \(n>3\)?

## Literature
- Background notes uniqueness for \(n=4\) and reports computational evidence of uniqueness (up to graph isomorphism) for many \(5\le n\le 21\).
- Congruence-level multiplicity remains open.

## Our Approaches / What Is Proven
- Ran deeper lattice-based search for high unit-edge configurations up to \(n=8\), with local improvement steps.
- Tracked multiplicity at the top via two coarse invariants:
  - full distance-multiset signature,
  - unit-distance graph signature (degree/triangle profile).

## Computation-Guided Observations
- Best unit-edge counts found rise from 4 (\(n=4\)) to 10 (\(n=8\)) in this search setting.
- At optimum, multiplicity of signatures is:
  - small for \(n=4,5\),
  - clearly non-unique by these coarse signatures for \(n=6,7,8\).
- This is suggestive but not decisive for true incongruence counting.

## Status
- Open.
