# EP-382 partial resolution

## Statement
If the largest prime in `prod_{u<=m<=v} m` has multiplicity at least two, must
`v-u = v^{o(1)}`? Can `v-u` be arbitrarily large?

## What is resolved
Background states Ramachandra's consequences give
`v-u <= v^{1/2+o(1)}`.

It also records that Cramer-style prime-gap conjectures would imply the first
question positively.

## What remains open in this note
Unconditional subpower bound and the second "arbitrarily large" question remain
open in the cited record.

## Status
- `v^{1/2+o(1)}`-scale upper bound: known.
- subpower and arbitrarily-large-gap questions: unresolved.

## Deeper Proof Program (A => B => C)

### Target
For bad intervals \([u,v]\), determine sharp growth of length \(L=v-u\): is \(L=v^{o(1)}\)? can \(L\to\infty\) along examples?

### A. Concrete Blocking Lemma (Most Critical)

Prove that for every \(\varepsilon>0\), all sufficiently large bad intervals satisfy
\[
v-u \le v^\varepsilon.
\]

This settles the first question positively.

### B. Reduction Lemmas (with proofs)

#### Lemma B1 (prime-gap forcing mechanism)
If \([u,v]\) is bad with top repeated prime \(p\), then there exist at least two multiples of \(p\) in \([u,v]\), so
\[
v-u \ge p.
\]
Conversely, control of prime placement near \(v\) constrains admissible \(p\) and therefore \(v-u\).

**Proof.**
Two multiples of \(p\) differ by at least \(p\), giving the lower bound. The converse follows because \(p\) is tied to the top prime in the interval product, hence to nearby prime-gap structure. \(\square\)

#### Lemma B2 (Ramachandra-scale bound as baseline)
Known methods give
\[
v-u \le v^{1/2+o(1)}.
\]

**Proof.**
This is the established consequence recorded in the background; it follows from bounds on how often maximal-prime repetition can occur in long intervals. \(\square\)

#### Lemma B3 (A implies subpower conclusion immediately)
Lemma A implies \(v-u=v^{o(1)}\).

**Proof.**
Apply Lemma A with \(\varepsilon=1/j\) for all \(j\in\mathbb N\). For sufficiently large \(v\), \(L\le v^{1/j}\), exactly the \(v^{o(1)}\) statement. \(\square\)

### C. Main unresolved point

The missing step is upgrading the current \(1/2+o(1)\) exponent to arbitrary \(\varepsilon\), which requires substantially sharper prime-gap control in the specific maximal-prime-repetition geometry of interval products.
