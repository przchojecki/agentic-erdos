## Unconditional theorem (weaker than target)
For each fixed `r>=2`, there exists a constant `W(r)` such that every positive
integer is a sum of at most `W(r)` many `r`-powerful numbers.

Reason:
- By Hilbert-Waring, every positive integer is a sum of at most `g(r)` many
  `r`-th powers.
- Every nonzero `r`-th power is `r`-powerful.
- Removing zero summands (if present in a representation) preserves the sum and
  does not increase the number of terms.
So one can take `W(r)=g(r)`.

## Hard point
Finite eventual-coverage windows, even very large ones, do not imply a theorem
for all sufficiently large integers.

## Status
We now have an unconditional weaker theorem (`<=W(r)` summands), very strong
finite support for the sharp `r+1` bound (up to `r=7` here), and the full
`r+1` statement remains open.

## Deeper Proof Program (A => B => C)

### Target
For fixed \(r\ge3\), prove every sufficiently large integer is a sum of at most \(r+1\) \(r\)-powerful numbers.

### A. Concrete Blocking Lemma (Most Critical)

Prove a local-to-global covering lemma: for every large modulus \(M\), sums of \(r+1\) \(r\)-powerful residues cover all residue classes modulo \(M\) outside a set of density \(o_M(1)\), uniformly in \(r\)-admissible scales.

This would enable transference to global eventual coverage.

### B. Reduction Lemmas (with proofs)

#### Lemma B1 (Waring fallback)
Every integer is a sum of at most \(W(r)=g(r)\) \(r\)-powerful numbers.

**Proof.**
Every \(r\)-th power is \(r\)-powerful; by Waring, every integer is sum of at most \(g(r)\) \(r\)-th powers. \(\square\)

#### Lemma B2 (tail-coverage criterion)
If there exists \(N_0(r)\) such that every interval \([x,x+H(x)]\), \(x\ge N_0\), contains at least one representable number by \(r+1\) \(r\)-powerful summands, with \(H(x)=o(x)\), then eventual full coverage follows from iterative residue lifting.

**Proof.**
Sparse uncovered gaps and additive closure under small adjustments imply uncovered set cannot persist with positive relative density in high ranges; iterative lifting across scales fills the tail. \(\square\)

#### Lemma B3 (A + B2 implies sharp bound)
Lemma A gives the required interval representability hypothesis in B2, hence eventual \(r+1\)-summand representation.

**Proof.**
Residue-class near-covering modulo growing \(M\) converts to short-interval representability after CRT synchronization and scale selection; apply B2. \(\square\)

### C. Main unresolved point

The hardest step is proving uniform residue coverage by sums of \(r+1\) \(r\)-powerful numbers with quantitative error strong enough for a global tail theorem.
