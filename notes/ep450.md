How large must `y=y(\epsilon,n)` be so that integers in `(x,x+y)` having a
divisor in `(n,2n)` are at most `\epsilon y`? (Quantifier on `x` is subtle.)

## Deeper Proof Program (A => B => C)

### Target
Determine minimal \(y=y(\varepsilon,n)\) such that numbers in \((x,x+y)\) having a divisor in \((n,2n)\) are at most \(\varepsilon y\), with careful quantifiers in \(x\).

### A. Concrete Blocking Lemma (Most Critical)

Prove a short-interval divisor-density theorem:
\[
\frac1y\#\{m\in(x,x+y]:\exists d\in(n,2n),\ d\mid m\}
=\mathfrak d(n)+O(E(x,y,n)),
\]
uniform in \(x\), with explicit error \(E\) small only above a sharp \(y\)-threshold.

### B. Reduction Lemmas (with proofs)

#### Lemma B1 (indicator decomposition)
Define \(1_D(m)\) for “\(m\) has divisor in \((n,2n)\)”. Then interval count is \(\sum_{x<m\le x+y}1_D(m)\).

**Proof.**
By definition of indicator. \(\square\)

#### Lemma B2 (inclusion-exclusion over divisors)
Average of \(1_D\) is approximated by union of arithmetic progressions modulo \(d\in(n,2n)\), with overlaps controlled by lcm terms.

**Proof.**
Use inclusion-exclusion for divisibility events \(d\mid m\). \(\square\)

#### Lemma B3 (A implies \(y(\varepsilon,n)\) scale)
If A gives uniform approximation with error \(<\varepsilon-\mathfrak d(n)\), then corresponding \(y\) is admissible threshold.

**Proof.**
Plug A into interval average and compare with \(\varepsilon\). \(\square\)

### C. Main unresolved point

The bottleneck is obtaining strong uniformity in \(x\) for short intervals where divisor-event correlations are large.
