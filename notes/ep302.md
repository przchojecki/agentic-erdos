# EP-302 partial resolution

## Statement
Let `f(N)` be the largest size of `A subseteq {1,...,N}` with no distinct
`a,b,c in A` satisfying

`1/a = 1/b + 1/c`.

Question includes: is `f(N) = (1/2+o(1))N`?

## What is resolved
Background already gives stronger lower bounds:
- `f(N) >= (5/8+o(1))N` (Cambie construction in background);
- and upper bound `f(N) <= (9/10+o(1))N` (van Doorn, as recorded in background).

Therefore the specific subquestion `f(N)=(1/2+o(1))N` is not compatible with the
recorded lower bound and is effectively ruled out by the provided background.

## What remains open in this note
The true asymptotic constant (or sharper asymptotic description) remains open
between the current lower and upper bounds listed in background.

## Status
- half-density guess: false from recorded lower bound.
- exact asymptotic value: unresolved.

## Lemma Chain (with Proofs)

### Lemma 1 (Equivalent Diophantine form)
For positive integers \(a,b,c\),
\[
\frac1a=\frac1b+\frac1c
\iff
bc=a(b+c).
\]

\[
\textit{Proof.}
\]
Multiply both sides by \(abc\):
\[
bc=ac+ab=a(b+c).
\]
Reverse implication follows by division through \(abc\neq0\).
\(\square\)

### Lemma 2 (Parametric divisor form)
Equation \(bc=a(b+c)\) is equivalent to
\[
(b-a)(c-a)=a^2.
\]

\[
\textit{Proof.}
\]
Expand:
\[
(b-a)(c-a)=bc-a(b+c)+a^2.
\]
Using \(bc=a(b+c)\), RHS equals \(a^2\). Conversely, rearranging gives the
original equation.
\(\square\)

### Lemma 3 (Finite counter to half-density claim)
If \(f(N)\ge \beta N\) for some \(N\) and \(\beta>1/2\), then the asymptotic claim
\(f(N)=(1/2+o(1))N\) is false.

\[
\textit{Proof.}
\]
The claim implies \(f(N)/N\to 1/2\). Existence of infinitely many (or even a
proven lower asymptotic \((5/8+o(1))N\)) contradicts convergence to \(1/2\).
\(\square\)

### Lemma 4 (Reduction of open part)
Given known bounds
\[
\frac58+o(1)\le \frac{f(N)}N\le \frac9{10}+o(1),
\]
the remaining problem is exactly to improve either endpoint toward a common
constant.

\[
\textit{Proof.}
\]
Direct interval squeeze: a limit/asymptotic constant is determined once upper and
lower bounds match.
\(\square\)
