## Lemma Chain (with Proofs)

### Lemma 1 (Residue form of distinct sums for \(A=\{1\}\cup h\mathbb N\))
Any distinct-summand sum from \(A\) is congruent mod \(h\) to either \(0\) or \(1\).

\[
\textit{Proof.}
\]
All elements except \(1\) are \(0\pmod h\). Distinctness allows using \(1\) at
most once, so residue is \(0\) (without \(1\)) or \(1\) (with \(1\)).
\(\square\)

### Lemma 2 (Immediate restricted-order failure)
If \(h\ge3\), residues \(2,\dots,h-1\) are never attained by distinct sums, so
restricted order does not exist.

\[
\textit{Proof.}
\]
By Lemma 1 only residues \(0,1\) occur; infinitely many integers in other
residues remain uncovered.
\(\square\)

### Lemma 3 (Unrestricted-order possibility)
Unrestricted sums may reuse \(1\), so all residues mod \(h\) can be reached by
taking enough \(1\)'s plus multiples of \(h\). Hence modular obstruction from
Lemma 1 disappears.

\[
\textit{Proof.}
\]
Given residue \(r\), use \(r\) copies of \(1\) (mod \(h\)), then adjust magnitude
with \(h\)-multiples.
\(\square\)
