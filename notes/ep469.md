# EP-469 deeper attempt

## Statement
Let `A` be primitive pseudoperfect numbers. Does `sum_{n in A} 1/n` converge?

## Exact finite computation in this attempt
I scanned all `n<=120000` with exact subset-sum tests over proper divisors, then filtered
primitive cases.

Data file:
- `data/ep469_primitive_pseudoperfect_scan.json`

Results in range:
- primitive count: `541`
- partial reciprocal sum: `~0.340481`

## Interpretation
This gives exact finite data only. It supports a slow-growing partial sum in this range, but does
not prove convergence/divergence.

## Deeper Proof Program (A => B => C)

### Target
Decide convergence of
\[
\sum_{n\in \mathcal A}\frac1n
\]
where \(\mathcal A\) is primitive pseudoperfect numbers.

### A. Concrete Blocking Lemma (Most Critical)

Prove counting upper bound
\[
\#\{n\le x:\ n\in\mathcal A\}\le x^{1-\delta}
\]
for some \(\delta>0\), or stronger logarithmic sparsity.

### B. Reduction Lemmas (with proofs)

#### Lemma B1 (partial summation criterion)
If \(A(x)=\#(\mathcal A\cap[1,x])\), then
\[
\sum_{\substack{n\in\mathcal A\\ n\le X}}\frac1n
=\frac{A(X)}{X}+\int_1^X \frac{A(t)}{t^2}\,dt.
\]

**Proof.**
Apply Abel summation to indicator sequence of \(\mathcal A\). \(\square\)

#### Lemma B2 (convergence test)
If \(A(x)\ll x^{1-\delta}\), then \(\sum_{n\in\mathcal A}1/n\) converges.

**Proof.**
Insert bound into B1: integral \(\int t^{-1-\delta}dt\) converges. \(\square\)

#### Lemma B3 (A implies convergence)
Lemma A directly implies B2, hence convergence.

**Proof.**
Immediate substitution. \(\square\)

### C. Main unresolved point

The bottleneck is proving strong sparsity for primitive pseudoperfect numbers, which combine subset-sum and divisor-structure constraints.
