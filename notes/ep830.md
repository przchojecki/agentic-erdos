# EP-830

## Problem Statement
Two integers $a,b\in\mathbb N$ are amicable if
$$\sigma(a)=\sigma(b)=a+b.$$ 
Are there infinitely many amicable pairs? If $A(x)$ counts amicable $1\le a\le b\le x$, is it true that
$$A(x)>x^{1-o(1)}?$$

## Literature
- Erdos proved $A(x)=o(x)$.
- Pomerance established strong sublinear upper bounds, including
  $$A(x) \le x\exp\!\bigl(-(\tfrac12+o(1))(\log x\log\log x)^{1/2}\bigr).$$
- The infinitude question remains open.

## Our Approaches / What Is Proven
- Built a deeper exact finite scan up to $2\cdot 10^6$ using a divisor-sum sieve.
- Enumerated amicable pairs and profiled $A(x)$ across multiple scales.

## Computation-Guided Observations
- Found 55 amicable pairs with smaller member $a\le 2\cdot10^6$.
- The density proxy $A(x)/x$ decreases on this range (from $1.6\times10^{-4}$ at $x=5\cdot10^4$ to $2.75\times10^{-5}$ at $x=2\cdot10^6$).
- Finite data remains far from the near-linear growth target.
