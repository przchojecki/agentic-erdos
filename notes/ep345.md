# EP-345 partial

## Statement
For `A_k={1^k,2^k,...}`, let `T(n^k)` be the threshold of completeness.
Are there infinitely many `k` with `T(n^k) > T(n^{k+1})`?

## Attempt in this batch
I reviewed the strongest concrete values recorded in the background and compared
their monotonic pattern.

## Result
Known values listed in background:
- `T(n)=1`
- `T(n^2)=128`
- `T(n^3)=12758`
- `T(n^4)=5134240`
- `T(n^5)=67898771`

These initial values are increasing, so no early example of
`T(n^k) > T(n^{k+1})` is visible yet.

## Hard point
The question is about infinitely many reversals at large `k`, far beyond the
currently known finite data.

## New Experiments
- 2026-03-05T14:58:24.767Z: enforced deep standalone finite threshold-proxy scan (k=2..6, limits up to 8.5e6, runtime 16.59s).
- Finite signal: no reversal pattern was detected in this proxy; observed largest-missing values remained nondecreasing with k across all tested limits.
- Proof direction update: computations support early monotonic growth of threshold complexity in k; any true infinite reversal mechanism would need a genuinely asymptotic effect absent from these finite windows.

## Deeper Proof Program (A => B => C)

### Target
Decide whether infinitely many \(k\) satisfy
\[
T(n^k)>T(n^{k+1}),
\]
where \(T(\cdot)\) is the completeness threshold.

### Step A: Cross-\(k\) transfer inequalities
Relate representability by \(k\)-th powers and \((k+1)\)-th powers through
explicit comparison maps.

### Step B: Threshold monotonicity defect analysis
Identify mechanisms causing possible reversals despite apparent early monotonicity.

### Step C: Infinite occurrence vs eventual monotonicity
Prove one of: infinitely many reversals, or eventual one-direction monotonicity.

### Concrete Blocking Lemma (Most Critical)
Need an asymptotic comparison lemma controlling distortion between \(k\)-power and
\((k+1)\)-power sumsets near their thresholds.

## Lemma Chain (with Proofs)

### Lemma 1 (Threshold characterization)
For sequence \(A\), \(T(A)\) equals
\[
1+\max\{m\in\mathbb N:\ m\ \text{is not representable by finite sums of distinct
elements of }A\}
\]
when the maximum exists.

\[
\textit{Proof.}
\]
By definition of threshold as first point after which all integers are
representable.
\(\square\)

### Lemma 2 (Immediate reversal criterion)
If for some \(k\), there exists \(x\) not representable by \((k+1)\)-powers but
every integer \(>x\) is representable by \((k+1)\)-powers, and \(x<T(n^k)\), then
\[
T(n^{k+1})\le x+1<T(n^k).
\]

\[
\textit{Proof.}
\]
First inequality from Lemma 1 for \(k+1\). Second is assumption.
\(\square\)

### Lemma 3 (Reduction of EP-345)
To prove infinitely many reversals, it suffices to produce infinitely many \(k\)
satisfying Lemma 2's hypothesis.

\[
\textit{Proof.}
\]
Each such \(k\) yields one strict reversal by Lemma 2.
\(\square\)
