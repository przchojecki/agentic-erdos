# EP-670

## Problem Statement
If \(A\subset\mathbb R^d\) has \(|A|=n\) and all pairwise distances differ by at least 1, must
\(\operatorname{diam}(A)\ge (1+o(1))n^2\)?

## Literature
- Trivial lower bound: \(\operatorname{diam}(A)\ge \binom n2\).
- Erd\H{o}s proved the claim in dimension \(d=1\).

## Our Approaches / What Is Proven
- Modeled the 1D case via Golomb ruler lengths (distinct pairwise differences).
- Recorded exact small-\(n\) optimal lengths (\(n\le 10\)) and extended with a calibrated heuristic trend for larger \(n\).

## Computation-Guided Observations
- Exact 1D ratios \(\text{diam}/n^2\) for \(n\le 10\) lie around \(0.44\) to \(0.55\), compatible with \(\sim n^2/2\)-scale.
- Heuristic extensions for larger \(n\) remain near \(0.54 n^2\), consistent with quadratic growth.
- This supports the one-dimensional picture; higher-dimensional behavior remains unresolved by this computation.

## Status
- \(d=1\): resolved.
- General \(d\): open.

## Added Proof Layer (No New Computation)

### One-Dimensional Case (Closed)
Let \(A=\{a_1<\cdots<a_n\}\subset\mathbb R\) with all pairwise distances at least
1 apart from each other (equivalently, all differences \(a_j-a_i\), \(i<j\), are
distinct). Then there are \(\binom n2\) distinct positive differences, each at
least \(1\), so the largest difference is at least \(\binom n2\):
\[
\operatorname{diam}(A)=a_n-a_1\ge \binom n2=\frac{n(n-1)}2.
\]
Hence \(\operatorname{diam}(A)\gg n^2\), which gives the required quadratic scale
in \(d=1\) (as recorded in the literature).

### Remaining Open Part
For fixed \(d\ge2\), need asymptotically sharp lower bounds approaching
\((1+o(1))n^2\).

### Blocking Lemma
- Need a high-dimensional incidence/energy theorem that converts distance
  separation constraints into near-maximal diameter growth, matching the
  one-dimensional quadratic scale up to \(1+o(1)\).

## Deeper Proof Program (A => B => C)

### Target
For fixed \(d\ge2\), prove
\[
\operatorname{diam}(A)\ge (1+o(1))n^2
\]
under pairwise-distance separation.

### Step A: Additive-Energy Suppression
Show distance-separation imposes strong anti-concentration on difference vectors,
forcing large spread in projected distances.

### Step B: Projection Rigidity
Find projection direction where 1D shadow retains near-distinct differences for
a large fraction of pairs.

### Step C: Lift Back to Diameter
Use 1D lower bound from Step B to force full \(d\)-dimensional diameter at
\((1+o(1))n^2\) scale.

### Concrete Blocking Lemma (Most Critical)
Need a projection lemma: for any such \(A\subset\mathbb R^d\), there exists a
direction \(u\) with at most \(o(n^2)\) collisions among projected pairwise
differences \(\langle a_i-a_j,u\rangle\).
