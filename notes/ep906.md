# EP-906

## Problem Statement
Does there exist a nonzero entire function $f$ such that for every infinite increasing sequence
$$
n_1<n_2<\cdots,
$$
the set
$$
\{z\in\mathbb C: f^{(n_k)}(z)=0\ \text{for some }k\}
$$
is dense in $\mathbb C$?

## Literature
- Existing in-repo commentary indicates the as-written formulation is likely too weak/trivial and that intended variants usually impose extra constraints (e.g. transcendental/non-polynomial behavior).

## Our Approaches / What Is Proven
- Direct witness for the as-written existential statement:
  - choose $f(z)=1$ (nonzero entire), then for every $m\ge 1$, $f^{(m)}\equiv 0$.
  - hence for any infinite increasing sequence $(n_k)$ of positive integers, the derivative-zero set is all of $\mathbb C$.
- Added standalone script/data sanity check over multiple derivative-order sequences and a finite complex grid.

## Computation-Guided Observations
- Finite checks are consistent with the exact argument: all tested derivative orders evaluate to zero identically for $f(z)=1$.

## Status
- As written: solved trivially (positive existential answer).
- Intended stronger variant with additional constraints remains separate/open.
