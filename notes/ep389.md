# EP-389 deep attempt with bounded computation

## Statement
For each `n>=1`, does there exist `k` such that
`n(n+1)...(n+k-1)` divides `(n+k)...(n+2k-1)`?

## Attempt route
Checked prime-valuation divisibility condition with bounded search over `k`.

cap for `n=6` through `30`.

## Obstacle

## Deeper Proof Program (A => B => C)

### Target
For each \(n\ge1\), decide existence of \(k\) such that
\[
\prod_{i=0}^{k-1}(n+i)\ \bigm|\ \prod_{i=0}^{k-1}(n+k+i).
\]

### A. Concrete Blocking Lemma (Most Critical)

Prove a valuation-dominance criterion: for each prime \(p\),
\[
v_p\!\left(\prod_{i=0}^{k-1}(n+k+i)\right)-v_p\!\left(\prod_{i=0}^{k-1}(n+i)\right)\ge0
\]
once \(k\) lies in an explicit progression depending on \(n\), simultaneously for all \(p\).

### B. Reduction Lemmas (with proofs)

#### Lemma B1 (factorial ratio rewrite)
\[
\prod_{i=0}^{k-1}(n+i)=\frac{(n+k-1)!}{(n-1)!},\quad
\prod_{i=0}^{k-1}(n+k+i)=\frac{(n+2k-1)!}{(n+k-1)!}.
\]

**Proof.**
Apply consecutive-product factorial identities. \(\square\)

#### Lemma B2 (divisibility as valuation inequalities)
Desired divisibility is equivalent to
\[
v_p((n+2k-1)!)+v_p((n-1)!)\ge 2v_p((n+k-1)!)
\]
for every prime \(p\).

**Proof.**
Substitute B1 and compare \(p\)-adic valuations. \(\square\)

#### Lemma B3 (A implies existence of witness \(k\))
If A provides a \(k\) making all inequalities in B2 hold, divisibility follows.

**Proof.**
B2 is equivalent characterization. \(\square\)

### C. Main unresolved point

The bottleneck is achieving simultaneous valuation dominance across all primes without requiring prohibitively large \(k\).
