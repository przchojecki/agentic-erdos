## Deeper Proof Program (A => B => C)

### Target
For fixed \(k\), show infinitely many primes \(p\) satisfy
\[
P\!\Big(\prod_{i=0}^k (p^2+i)\Big)=p.
\]

### A. Concrete Blocking Lemma (Most Critical)

For fixed \(k\), prove a positive-density prime set \(\mathcal P_k\) such that for every \(p\in\mathcal P_k\),
\[
P(p^2+i)\le p\quad\text{for all }0\le i\le k.
\]
This immediately gives infinitude.

### B. Reduction Lemmas (with proofs)

#### Lemma B1 (factorwise criterion)
For prime \(p\),
\[
P\!\Big(\prod_{i=0}^k (p^2+i)\Big)=p
\iff
\max_{0\le i\le k}P(p^2+i)\le p
\]
because \(p^2\) contributes prime \(p\), so equality of maxima is forced.

**Proof.**
Largest prime factor of a product is the maximum of largest prime factors of factors. One factor is \(p^2\), whose largest prime factor is exactly \(p\). Hence product-largest-prime equals \(p\) exactly when all other factors have largest prime \(\le p\). \(\square\)

#### Lemma B2 (congruence obstruction form)
If some \(p^2+i\) has a prime factor \(q>p\), then \(p\) lies in a residue class modulo \(q\) solving
\[
t^2\equiv -i \pmod q.
\]

**Proof.**
From \(q\mid p^2+i\), reduce modulo \(q\): \(p^2\equiv -i\pmod q\). Thus \(p\bmod q\) is one of the square roots of \(-i\), if they exist. \(\square\)

#### Lemma B3 (sieve-to-infinitude bridge)
If the union over \(i\le k\) and \(q>p\) of the residue obstructions in Lemma B2 has lower natural density \(<1\) among primes \(p\), then infinitely many good \(p\) exist.

**Proof.**
Primes outside that obstruction union satisfy \(P(p^2+i)\le p\) for all \(i\), hence are good by Lemma B1. Positive lower density implies infinitude. \(\square\)

### C. Main unresolved point

The hard step is a uniform sieve bound controlling the infinite family of quadratic residue obstructions \(p^2\equiv -i\pmod q\) with threshold \(q>p\), simultaneously for all \(i\le k\).
