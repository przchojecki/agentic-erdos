## Deeper Proof Program (A => B => C)

### Target
For \(\binom{n}{k}\), force existence of a divisor in intervals like \((cn,n]\) (or \((n-k,n]\)).

### A. Concrete Blocking Lemma (Most Critical)

Prove a prime-support concentration lemma: for each fixed \(c<1\), sufficiently large \((n,k)\) force at least one prime \(p\in(cn,n]\) with
\[
v_p\!\binom{n}{k}>0.
\]

### B. Reduction Lemmas (with proofs)

#### Lemma B1 (interval-divisor equivalence via primes)
If some prime \(p\in(cn,n]\) divides \(\binom{n}{k}\), then \(\binom{n}{k}\) has a divisor in \((cn,n]\).

**Proof.**
Take divisor \(d=p\). \(\square\)

#### Lemma B2 (valuation criterion)
\[
v_p\!\binom{n}{k}>0
\iff
\text{a carry occurs in base }p\text{ when adding }k\text{ and }n-k.
\]

**Proof.**
Kummer theorem for binomial valuations. \(\square\)

#### Lemma B3 (A implies interval divisor theorem)
Lemma A gives such a prime \(p\), then B1 yields desired divisor.

**Proof.**
Direct composition A \(\Rightarrow\) B1. \(\square\)

### C. Main unresolved point

The bottleneck is proving large-prime divisibility in specific near-top intervals uniformly over all relevant \((n,k)\).
