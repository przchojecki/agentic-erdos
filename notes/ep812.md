# EP-812

## Problem Statement
Is it true that $\frac{R(n+1)}{R(n)}\ge 1+c$ for some absolute constant $c>0$ and all sufficiently large $n$? Is it true that $R(n+1)-R(n)\gg n^2$?

## Literature
- Burr-Erdos-Faudree-Schelp (1989):
  $$R(n+1)-R(n)\ge 4n-8\quad(n\ge 2).$$
- Background note: known two-step growth of the form
  $$R(n+2)-R(n)\gg n^{2-o(1)}.$$

## Our Approaches / What Is Proven
- Ran a quantitative implication scan of the known linear one-step lower bound.
- Constructed the minimal sequence consistent with $R(n+1)-R(n)\ge 4n-8$ and measured:
  - implied ratio increment $R(n+1)/R(n)-1$;
  - linear-vs-quadratic gap scale $(4n-8)/n^2$.
- The computation is diagnostic: it clarifies what current one-step input can and cannot imply.

## Computation-Guided Observations
- In the minimal model, implied ratio increments decay (about $0.013$ by $n\approx 150$).
- The normalized one-step gap $(4n-8)/n^2\asymp 4/n\to 0$, far from quadratic-scale growth.
- This supports that present one-step lower bounds are insufficient for either target claim.
