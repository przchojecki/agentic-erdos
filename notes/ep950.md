# EP-950

## Problem Statement
Let
$$
f(n)=\sum_{p<n}\frac{1}{n-p},
$$
where the sum is over primes $p$.
Is it true that
$$
\liminf f(n)=1,\qquad \limsup f(n)=\infty?
$$
Is it true that $f(n)=o(\log\log n)$ for all $n$?

## Literature
- Known background includes strong moment information (average and second moment asymptotics) and several conditional consequences from short-interval prime distribution hypotheses.
- Pointwise liminf/limsup and sharp universal growth control remain open.

## Our Approaches / What Is Proven
- Added standalone deep finite computation for
$$
f(n)=\sum_{p<n}\frac1{n-p}
$$
up to $N=300{,}000$.
- Computed full value profile, checkpoint moment summaries, and extreme-value records.

## Computation-Guided Observations
- At $N=300{,}000$:
  - empirical mean over $n\le N$ is about $1.0617$,
  - empirical second moment is about $1.2328$,
  - max observed value is about $2.5535$ (at $n=284{,}750$).
- Max normalized by $\log\log N$ in this range is about $1.007$, indicating values can exceed the scale-1 baseline but finite data does not decide asymptotic limsup behavior.
- Finite profile is consistent with substantial fluctuations around mean level near 1, in line with known moment-level picture.
