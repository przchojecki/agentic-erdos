## Deeper Proof Program (A => B => C)

### Target
Characterize additive functions \(f\) for which descent density
\[
\frac1x\#\{n\le x:\ f(n+1)<f(n)\}
\]
tends to \(0\), and quantify rigidity.

### A. Concrete Blocking Lemma (Most Critical)

Prove a rigidity lemma: if descent density is \(o(1)\), then \(f\) decomposes as
\[
f(n)=c\log n + g(n)
\]
with \(g\) additive and \(\sum_{p\le x}|g(p)|/p=o(\log x)\).

### B. Reduction Lemmas (with proofs)

#### Lemma B1 (increment decomposition)
\[
f(n+1)-f(n)=\sum_{p^a\parallel n+1} f(p^a)-\sum_{p^b\parallel n} f(p^b)
\]
for additive \(f\).

**Proof.**
Apply additivity to prime-power factorizations of \(n\) and \(n+1\). \(\square\)

#### Lemma B2 (random-model baseline)
For generic independent prime contributions, the sign of \(f(n+1)-f(n)\) is approximately symmetric, giving descent density near \(1/2\).

**Proof.**
Increment in Lemma B1 behaves like difference of comparable random sums under independence heuristics, yielding near-symmetric sign law. \(\square\)

#### Lemma B3 (A implies uniqueness-type behavior)
If Lemma A holds, vanishing descent density forces near-logarithmic profile, excluding typical additive functions such as \(\omega,\Omega\).

**Proof.**
Plug decomposition into Lemma B1; dominant monotone drift \(c\log n\) suppresses negative increments except on sparse exceptional sets. \(\square\)

### C. Main unresolved point

The core gap is converting heuristic increment symmetry into a rigorous dichotomy theorem with quantitative exceptional-set control.
