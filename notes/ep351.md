## Deeper Proof Program (A => B => C)

### Target
Classify polynomials \(p\) for which subset sums of
\[
\left\{p(n)+\frac1n\right\}_{n\ge1}
\]
exhibit strong completeness / near-integer coverage behavior.

### Step A: Integer-fraction split
Write subset sums as
\[
\sum_{n\in S}p(n) + \sum_{n\in S}\frac1n.
\]
Separate hard part (harmonic fractional control) from polynomial integer growth.

### Step B: Fractional cancellation mechanism
Determine when reciprocal part can hit prescribed residues modulo \(1\) densely
enough to fill integer targets.

### Step C: Degree-dependent criterion
Show low-degree \(p\) satisfy Step B robustly, while higher-degree need extra
structure.

### Concrete Blocking Lemma (Most Critical)
Need a quantitative equidistribution/cancellation theorem for finite reciprocal
subsum residues compatible with polynomial integer shifts.

## Lemma Chain (with Proofs)

### Lemma 1 (Integer shift invariance)
For integer-valued \(p\) on \(\mathbb N\), integrality of subset sum
\[
\sum_{n\in S}\left(p(n)+\frac1n\right)
\]
is equivalent to integrality of \(\sum_{n\in S}1/n\) shifted by an integer.

\[
\textit{Proof.}
\]
\(\sum_{n\in S}p(n)\in\mathbb Z\), so adding/removing it does not affect fractional
part.
\(\square\)

### Lemma 2 (Finite-window transfer)
If in a window \(W\) the reciprocal subsums realize all fractions in an interval
of width \(\eta\) modulo \(1\), then adding integer polynomial part yields an
integer run of comparable length in total sums.

\[
\textit{Proof sketch.}
\]
Fix integer part from chosen polynomial indices; vary reciprocal part across dense
fractional grid to cross consecutive integer thresholds.
\(\square\)

### Lemma 3 (Reduction)
EP-351 reduces to proving strong fractional coverage for reciprocal subsums
associated with admissible index families determined by \(p\).

\[
\textit{Proof.}
\]
By Lemma 1, integer component is neutral modulo \(1\); Lemma 2 converts
fractional coverage into integer-value coverage.
\(\square\)
