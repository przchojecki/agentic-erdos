## Deeper Proof Program (A => B => C)

### Target
For all \(n\ge2\), prove
\[
\lim_{k\to\infty}\sigma_k(n)^{1/k}=\infty,\qquad \sigma_{k+1}(n)=\sigma(\sigma_k(n)).
\]

### A. Concrete Blocking Lemma (Most Critical)

Establish an eventual multiplicative growth inequality:
\[
\sigma_{k+1}(n)\ge \sigma_k(n)^{1+\epsilon_k},
\]
with \(\epsilon_k>0\) and \(\sum_k \epsilon_k=\infty\) along each trajectory.

### B. Reduction Lemmas (with proofs)

#### Lemma B1 (basic lower bound)
\[
\sigma(m)\ge m+1\quad(m\ge1),
\]
strict for \(m>1\).

**Proof.**
Divisors include \(1\) and \(m\), with possibly more positive terms. \(\square\)

#### Lemma B2 (iteration monotonicity)
For \(n\ge2\), \(\sigma_k(n)\) is strictly increasing eventually.

**Proof.**
By B1, each step adds at least \(1\), and values remain \(>1\). \(\square\)

#### Lemma B3 (A implies root divergence)
Taking logs of A:
\[
\log \sigma_{k+1}\ge (1+\epsilon_k)\log \sigma_k.
\]
Summing multiplicative gains gives \(\log \sigma_k / k\to\infty\), hence \(\sigma_k^{1/k}\to\infty\).

**Proof.**
Iterate inequality and use divergence of \(\sum \epsilon_k\). \(\square\)

### C. Main unresolved point

The key obstacle is proving persistent superlinear gain along every orbit, despite occasional finite-step dips observed numerically.
