# EP-324 partial resolution

## Statement
Does there exist `f in Z[x]` such that all pair sums `f(a)+f(b)` for
`0<=a<b` are distinct?

## What is resolved
Background indicates that proving this for `f(x)=x^n` with `n>=5` would follow
from the Lander-Parkin-Selfridge conjecture.

A local computation in this workspace found no collisions for `f(x)=x^5` on the
range `0<=a<b<=1200` (720600 pairs checked).

## What remains open in this note
No unconditional proof is provided in the cited background.

## Status
- substantial heuristic and finite-range evidence: present.
- full theorem: unresolved.

## Deeper Proof Program (A => B => C)

### Target
Find \(f\in\mathbb Z[x]\) such that pair sums \(f(a)+f(b)\) (\(0\le a<b\)) are all
distinct.

### Step A: Collision equation reduction
Analyze Diophantine equation
\[
f(a)+f(b)=f(c)+f(d),\qquad \{a,b\}\ne\{c,d\},
\]
for candidate \(f\).

### Step B: Rigidity for power-like \(f\)
Prove that for \(f(x)=x^n\) (large \(n\)), nontrivial collisions are impossible.

### Step C: Integer-polynomial realization
Either prove one explicit \(f\) works unconditionally or derive from a known
Diophantine conjecture plus a verified implication.

### Concrete Blocking Lemma (Most Critical)
Need a uniform nontrivial-solution exclusion for
\[
a^n+b^n=c^n+d^n,\quad \{a,b\}\ne\{c,d\},
\]
in the relevant nonnegative range.

## Lemma Chain (with Proofs)

### Lemma 1 (Collision criterion for injectivity)
Pair-sum map \(\{a,b\}\mapsto f(a)+f(b)\) is injective iff the collision equation
above has only trivial solutions \(\{a,b\}=\{c,d\}\).

\[
\textit{Proof.}
\]
Injectivity failure is exactly existence of two distinct pairs with equal image.
\(\square\)

### Lemma 2 (Strict convexity monotonic ordering)
If \(f\) is strictly convex on \(\mathbb Z_{\ge0}\), then for fixed \(s=a+b\),
the function \(a\mapsto f(a)+f(s-a)\) is strictly increasing for
\(a\le s/2\).

\[
\textit{Proof.}
\]
Discrete derivative:
\[
[f(a+1)-f(a)]-[f(s-a)-f(s-a-1)].
\]
Strict convexity makes \(f(t+1)-f(t)\) strictly increasing in \(t\), so for
\(a<s/2\) this difference is positive.
\(\square\)

### Lemma 3 (Reduction)
If one proves that every nontrivial collision for candidate \(f\) would force an
equal-sum identity forbidden by Lemma 2 monotonicity (or by a stronger
Diophantine theorem), then \(f\) is a valid EP-324 witness.

\[
\textit{Proof.}
\]
By Lemma 1, absence of nontrivial collisions is equivalent to required property.
\(\square\)
