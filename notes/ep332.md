# EP-332 partial

## Statement
For `A subseteq N`, let `D(A)` be differences occurring infinitely often as
`a1-a2` with `a1,a2 in A`. Find sufficient conditions ensuring bounded gaps in
`D(A)`.

## Attempt in this batch
I ran finite proxies on several families (`squares`, `primes`, `powers of 2`,
random sparse/dense sets), tracking difference-frequency gap behavior.

Data file:
- `data/ep332_difference_set_gap_scan.json`

## Result
Finite scans show dense/random sets quickly produce very small difference gaps,
while sparse structured sets (e.g. powers of 2) keep large gaps under stricter
frequency thresholds.

This is consistent with the background point that positive density is a strong
sufficient condition.

## Hard point
Finite frequency proxies are not equivalent to the infinite-occurrence definition
of `D(A)`, so this does not produce new sharp sufficient criteria.

## Deeper Proof Program (A => B => C)

### Target
Find sufficient conditions on \(A\subset\mathbb N\) ensuring bounded gaps in
\[
D(A)=\{d\ge1:\ d=a_1-a_2\ \text{infinitely often with }a_i\in A\}.
\]

### Step A: Correlation lower bound hypothesis
Assume quantitative pair-correlation:
\[
|\{(x,x+d)\in A^2:\ x\le X\}|\ge cX
\]
for many \(d\) uniformly.

### Step B: Infinite-occurrence upgrade
Convert positive-density-in-\(X\) pair counts into \(d\in D(A)\).

### Step C: Gap control
Show every interval of \(d\)-values of bounded length contains one satisfying
Step A.

### Concrete Blocking Lemma (Most Critical)
Need a uniform correlation-to-coverage theorem: from averaged pair-correlation on
short \(d\)-intervals, deduce at least one infinitely-often difference in each.

## Lemma Chain (with Proofs)

### Lemma 1 (Infinite occurrences from linear counts)
If for fixed \(d\),
\[
\limsup_{X\to\infty}\#\{x\le X:\ x,x+d\in A\}=+\infty,
\]
then \(d\in D(A)\).

\[
\textit{Proof.}
\]
Unbounded count provides infinitely many distinct witness pairs.
\(\square\)

### Lemma 2 (Bounded-gap criterion)
If there exists \(L\) such that every interval \([t,t+L]\) contains some \(d\)
with \(d\in D(A)\), then \(D(A)\) has gaps at most \(L\).

\[
\textit{Proof.}
\]
By definition, no run of \(L+1\) consecutive integers can avoid \(D(A)\).
\(\square\)

### Lemma 3 (Reduction)
To prove bounded gaps in \(D(A)\), it suffices to establish an \(L\) for which
each \([t,t+L]\) contains a \(d\) satisfying Lemma 1.

\[
\textit{Proof.}
\]
Apply Lemma 2 once Lemma 1 conditions are verified interval-by-interval.
\(\square\)
