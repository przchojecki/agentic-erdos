## Deeper Proof Program (A => B => C)

### Target
Decide infinitude of primary pseudoperfect numbers \(m\) (equivalently EP-313
solutions).

### Step A: Divisor-sum reformulation
Use the equivalent identity
\[
\sum_{p\mid m}\frac1p+\frac1m=1
\]
for squarefree \(m\), translating the prime-reciprocal equation into a divisor
equation.

### Step B: Multiplicative extension criterion
Characterize when appending a new prime factor preserves the identity.

### Step C: Infinite chain vs obstruction
Either build an infinite extension chain or prove a terminal obstruction.

### Concrete Blocking Lemma (Most Critical)
Need an extension lemma with effective congruence control: given a solution \(m\),
existence/nonexistence of a prime \(q\nmid m\) such that \(mq\) is again primary
pseudoperfect.

## Lemma Chain (with Proofs)

### Lemma 1 (Equivalent divisor identity)
For squarefree \(m=\prod_{i=1}^r p_i\),
\[
\sum_{i=1}^r \frac1{p_i}=1-\frac1m
\iff
\sum_{p\mid m}\frac1p+\frac1m=1.
\]

\[
\textit{Proof.}
\]
Move \(1/m\) to the left-hand side.
\(\square\)

### Lemma 2 (Finite verification principle)
If a set \(M_0\) of candidate \(m\) is fully scanned up to bound \(X\), any
unseen solution must satisfy \(m>X\).

\[
\textit{Proof.}
\]
Immediate from exhaustive search correctness.
\(\square\)

### Lemma 3 (Reduction of infinitude)
Infinitude is equivalent to existence of arbitrarily large solutions.

\[
\textit{Proof.}
\]
For integer sets, infinite iff unbounded.
\(\square\)
