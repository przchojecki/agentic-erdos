# EP-948

## Problem Statement
Is there a function $f(n)$ and a $k$ such that in any $k$-coloring of the integers there exists a sequence
$$
a_1<a_2<\cdots
$$
with $a_n<f(n)$ for infinitely many $n$ and such that
$$
\left\{\sum_{i\in S} a_i : S\text{ finite}\right\}
$$
does not contain all colors?

## Literature
- A stronger monochromatic variant is known to fail (Galvin-type coloring argument).
- The weaker omission-of-at-least-one-color variant remains open.

## Our Approaches / What Is Proven
- Built finite proxy solver for random colorings on $[1,M]$:
  - fix omitted color,
  - search for increasing sequence with bound $a_i\le Bi$,
  - require every finite sum to stay within $[1,M]$ and avoid omitted color.
- Used depth-first exact search + binary search on achievable sequence length.
- Ran multiple parameter regimes for $k=3,4,5$ with dozens of trials each.

## Computation-Guided Observations
- Best finite lengths found in this run:
  - $k=3$ regimes: up to length $7$,
  - $k=4$: up to length $7$,
  - $k=5$: up to length $8$.
- Mean achievable length increased with number of colors in these finite random tests (for tested $(M,B)$), reflecting that omission constraints can be easier when target omitted color is rarer.
- These are finite random-coloring diagnostics, not asymptotic evidence for the infinite theorem.
