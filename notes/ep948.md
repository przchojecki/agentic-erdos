# EP-948 partial resolution

## Statement
Seek k and growth control a_n<f(n) infinitely often such that finite-sums set of
(a_n) misses at least one color in every k-coloring variant.

## What is resolved
Background gives a negative result for a stronger version:
Galvin constructed a 2-coloring showing one cannot always force monochromatic
finite-sums behavior in the originally stronger formulation.

## What remains open in this note
The weaker current version remains open (even for countably many colors per background).

## Status
- stronger monochromatic target is false.
- stated weaker variant remains open.

## Logical relation of formulations
Let \(FS(a_n)\) denote finite sums of a sequence \((a_n)\).

If one could force \(FS(a_n)\) to be monochromatic in every \(k\)-coloring, then automatically \(FS(a_n)\) misses at least \(k-1\) colors. Hence the monochromatic formulation implies the weaker color-omission formulation.

Therefore Galvin's counterexample to the monochromatic-forcing version only rules out that stronger implication target; it does not directly refute the weaker EP-948 statement.

So the open part is genuinely about omission without full monochromatic concentration.

## k=2 equivalence (exact)
For a 2-coloring \(c:\mathbb N\to\{1,2\}\), the condition
\[
\text{"\(FS(a_n)\) misses at least one color"}
\]
is equivalent to
\[
\text{"\(FS(a_n)\) is monochromatic"}.
\]
Reason: in two colors, missing one color means all points are in the other color.

Therefore:
- any 2-coloring counterexample to monochromaticity is also a counterexample to
  the "misses one color" property at \(k=2\);
- the unresolved part of EP-948 must be sought at \(k\ge 3\), where omission is
  genuinely weaker than monochromaticity.
